From television to the internet, every major technology shift of the last fifty years has given us a new reason to stay inside. A new way to connect with people through a screen instead of a room.

We've isolated ourselves. Not dramatically. Gradually. One convenient substitute at a time. The living room replaced the town square. The group chat replaced the table. We optimized for comfort and lost something we didn't know we needed.

Then we started feeling it.

Capitalism noticed before we did. The loneliness became a market. Bouldering gyms, padel courts, coworking spaces, fitness subscriptions. These aren't fitness decisions. They're loneliness decisions dressed up as lifestyle choices. We're paying monthly fees to be in the same room as other people. And it works.

I go to a bouldering gym. I play padel. There's a cafe I like to work from, not because the coffee is better than what I make at home, but because other people are there. I don't always talk to them. I don't need to. The presence is enough. You walk in, someone nods, you exist in a shared space. That feeling of **belonging**, quiet and unspoken, is what we're desperately short on.

Religion is making a comeback. Not necessarily because people are finding God, but because they're finding a room. A consistent group of people, a shared rhythm, a reason to show up at the same place at the same time. The structure matters. The ritual matters. The regularity of being recognized when you walk through the door.

I don't say that to look down on it. I say it because it makes perfect sense. Religion offers community, direction, and a sense of purpose in a world that's increasingly fragmented. CrossFit boxes, running clubs, and Sunday services have more in common than anyone admits. They all answer the same question: *where do I go to not be alone?*

Sociologists call these **third spaces**. Places that are neither home nor work. Barbershops, pubs, parks, post offices. The kinds of places where you run into the same people without planning to. Where interaction happens without an agenda or a subscription.

Most of those spaces are gone. What replaced them costs money and requires a signup form.

But we're finding our way back. Imperfectly, expensively, sometimes awkwardly. We're showing up. And showing up is the whole point.
