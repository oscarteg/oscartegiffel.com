As a software developer, my inability to focus wasn't just frustrating -- it was holding me back. I tried Pomodoro, GTD, countless YouTubers each promising the ultimate system. It wasn't until I found a content creator combining analog and digital methods that things started to click.

## The Evolution of a System

I started with rigid structures. Forced daily writing, strict schedules, too many apps. The result? Paralysis. The pressure to be perfectly productive led to doing nothing at all. The key wasn't following someone else's system to the letter. It was building something flexible that could adapt to how I actually work.

### The Digital-Analog Balance

The job is already screen-heavy. Adding more screens to the thinking side made things worse -- ideas slipping by, attention split, nothing settling. Writing by hand slowed me down in a way that turned out to be useful. Slower capture forced me to decide what was actually worth writing.

The split I landed on: analog for reflection and planning, digital for execution.

## The Analog Core

My analog system is four notebooks. Most of what shaped this came from [Parker's Pensees](https://www.youtube.com/c/ParkersPens%C3%A9es).

### 1. The Skill Acquisition Notebook

Currently dedicated to Japanese. As developers we're constantly learning new things, and having a dedicated physical space for focused practice makes a difference.

### 2. The Philosophical Journal

This is where I wrestle with complex ideas. I follow a structured approach:

1. Clearly state the problem or concept
2. List arguments and counter-arguments
3. Draw conclusions or identify areas for further exploration

This has genuinely improved how I break down complex problems and architecture decisions.

### 3. The Commonplace Book

A collection of things I find interesting or useful. Quotes, code snippets, architectural patterns, insights from tech talks. I go back to it whenever I'm stuck or need a spark for a new project.

### 4. The Pocket Notebook

A small Leuchtturm1917 that goes everywhere with me. Some of my best ideas started as quick notes jotted down during a coffee break or commute.

### The Compendium

A larger project, my compendium is a curated collection of life wisdom and technical knowledge. It's my personal encyclopedia, growing slowly but steadily with carefully selected insights.

## Digital Tools

### 1. Raindrop: The Knowledge Repository

My digital library for bookmarks. Articles, documentation, resources. Tagged and organized so I can actually find things again.

### 2. Capacities: The Digital Second Brain

My work note-taking tool. Using the second brain approach, I connect ideas across projects and technologies. I often find solutions to current problems buried in past notes.

### 3. Eraser.io: The Digital Whiteboard

For visualizing complex systems or explaining architectural decisions to teammates. The developer-friendly features make it much better than general-purpose drawing tools.


### 4. Things: The Task Manager

My to-do list is structured to balance immediate needs with long-term goals:

- Inbox: The catch-all for new tasks and ideas
- Today: Must-do items for the current day
- Anytime: Important but not time-sensitive tasks
- Someday: Long-term ideas and projects
- Projects: Multi-step tasks grouped by objective
- This Year/Next Year: Long-term planning and goal-setting

This keeps me focused on what needs to happen now without losing sight of the bigger goals.


### 5. Habitify: Building Better Dev Habits

For tracking habits. Right now I'm focusing on daily code reviews and regular stretching. The kind of stuff that's easy to skip but makes a real difference over time.

### 6. Day One: The Digital Time Capsule

Not forced journaling. More like capturing moments. Screenshots of major bug fixes, voice notes after important meetings, photos of whiteboard sessions. It's a nice way to look back and see how far you've come.

## Overcoming Challenges and Adapting the System

The biggest challenge was context switching between analog and digital. The solution? Mornings are for analog. Reflection and planning. Digital tools take over during work hours.

Consistency with the analog parts was hard too. What helped was linking new habits to existing ones. My commonplace book is now part of my morning coffee routine. No coffee, no skipping.

---

Productivity is deeply personal. This works for me, but it might not work for you. Start small -- a pocket notebook, a new task app -- and build from there.
