A friend plugged a monitor into his laptop and the resolution was wrong. He needed a driver update, which led to the question: *why does it need a driver?*

The analogy I used: a driver is a dictionary. It teaches your computer the language of a specific piece of hardware in his case, the graphics card, which in turn knows how to talk to the monitor.

Mostly accurate. More precisely a driver is a *phrasebook* than a dictionary -- the conversation goes both ways, in real time, in turn. But "dictionary" is good enough to get the idea across.

Then I started counting how many languages a laptop needs to speak in a normal day:

- USB, for keyboards, mice, chargers, webcams
- HDMI / DisplayPort, to push pixels to a screen
- Bluetooth, for wireless peripherals
- Wi-Fi and Ethernet, to find a network
- TCP / IP / DNS / HTTP / HTTPS / TLS, to use it
- A filesystem, to read and write files
- A dozen audio and video codecs (MP3, AAC, Opus, H.264, H.265, AV1)
- A dozen image formats (JPEG, PNG, WebP, AVIF)
- Character encoding (UTF-8 most of the time; the rest of the time it lies)
- Colour spaces (sRGB, Display P3) so red looks like red
- Compression formats (gzip, zstd) so things fit
- The graphics card's instruction set, so anything moves at all

Most of those dictionaries came pre-installed and you never think about them. You only notice the missing one -- the wrong resolution, the unrecognised device, the codec the browser doesn't know.

Drivers are the dictionaries you forgot were there.
